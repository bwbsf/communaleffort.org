# Burners Without Borders: Background and Chapter Collaboration Context

## Purpose

This document summarizes the history, structure, and chapter model of Burners Without Borders (BWB), with an emphasis on collaboration categories that make sense for local chapters.

It is not an outreach kit, legal guide, or agreement library. Operational drafting materials should live in separate implementation documents if they become useful later.

The next research phase is to compile a complete chapter inventory and, for each chapter, identify local organizations in each collaboration category that may be relevant partners.

## Summary

Burners Without Borders is a decentralized civic-action network connected to the broader Burning Man ecosystem. It supports volunteer-led projects, local chapters, working groups, and grassroots efforts related to disaster response, community resilience, public-space stewardship, mutual aid, education, sustainability, and locally defined civic needs.

BWB began after Hurricane Katrina in 2005, when Burning Man participants traveled from Black Rock City to the Gulf Coast and supported relief work around Biloxi, Mississippi. That early response work helped shape BWB's long-term identity: a network that brings creativity, participation, improvisation, and practical volunteer energy into community work.

Today, BWB is best understood as a support network rather than a centralized service provider. The headquarters function provides resources, connection, guidance, and program infrastructure, while chapters and working groups remain locally driven. This matters for collaboration planning because useful partnerships should strengthen local work without turning chapters into generic contractors or disconnected outside providers.

## History and Milestones

### 2005: Hurricane Katrina Origins

BWB traces its origin to the immediate aftermath of Hurricane Katrina in 2005. Burning Man participants left Black Rock City and traveled to the Gulf Coast, where they began relief work in and around Biloxi, Mississippi.

Early work included cleanup, supply distribution, improvised infrastructure, and direct support for affected communities. This origin story is central to BWB's identity because it links the network to practical response, volunteer coordination, and field improvisation.

### Post-Katrina: From Disaster Response to Local Civic Action

After the initial Gulf Coast work, BWB evolved from emergency response into a broader civic-action model. Its work expanded toward everyday community resilience, mutual aid, public-space stewardship, and locally initiated projects.

One early example was Cinco de Playa, a beach cleanup model that began at San Francisco's Ocean Beach and was later replicated in other locations. This helped show how BWB-style volunteer activation could be repeated outside an emergency context.

### International and Project-Based Growth

BWB later supported international projects, including earthquake recovery work in Pisco, Peru. That work included school reconstruction, temporary housing, sanitation infrastructure, youth programming, and coordination with local and international organizations.

The Pisco work is useful as a structural example because it shows BWB's ability to operate in cross-sector settings involving community leaders, public agencies, humanitarian organizations, and local successor organizations.

### Integration With Burning Man Project

BWB later became an official program of Burning Man Project, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit. Its current model combines a small support team with decentralized local leadership through chapters, working groups, and projects.

For local chapters, this means collaboration planning should distinguish between:

- what the chapter can do informally as a local volunteer group,
- what requires Burning Man Project or another legal entity,
- what should be handled by a local nonprofit, fiscal sponsor, or project partner,
- and what should remain a loose volunteer collaboration.

## Organizational Structure

BWB's structure is a network rather than a strict hierarchy.

- **Burning Man Project**: The nonprofit home for BWB.
- **Civic Activation / BWB support team**: The central support function that helps with resources, connection, guidance, and program infrastructure.
- **Advisory and experienced network members**: People who provide continuity, context, and subject-matter knowledge.
- **Chapters**: More formal local groups organized around place.
- **Working groups**: More informal or topic-based groups.
- **Projects**: Specific efforts initiated by volunteers, chapters, working groups, or aligned local collaborators.

The central support role is important, but the practical center of gravity is local. Chapters should therefore be treated as civic catalysts and volunteer mobilizers, not as branch offices executing a single top-down program.

## Chapter Activity Patterns

BWB chapters and projects tend to cluster around several recurring kinds of work.

### Mutual Aid and Direct Community Support

Chapters often help collect, sort, distribute, or deliver supplies. This can include food, hygiene supplies, blankets, emergency materials, or other locally requested items.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include existing service providers, neighborhood groups, mutual-aid networks, food banks, shelters, and community-based nonprofits.

### Disaster Response and Recovery

BWB's origin is in disaster response, and disaster-related work remains part of the network's identity. Chapters may support preparedness, post-disaster volunteer coordination, supply logistics, cleanup, or recovery projects.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include local emergency management offices, public agencies, resilience coalitions, neighborhood associations, community-based nonprofits, and groups already trusted by affected residents.

### Public-Space Stewardship

Examples include cleanup days, park stewardship, beautification, repair events, and public-space activation.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include parks departments, neighborhood associations, local environmental organizations, business corridors, schools, and arts or culture groups.

### Housing, Hygiene, and Unhoused Support

Some chapters support work related to unhoused neighbors, hygiene access, warming support, supply distribution, and service navigation.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include homelessness service providers, outreach teams, public health agencies, shelters, harm-reduction groups, food programs, and local mutual-aid networks.

### Education, Youth, and Skill-Sharing

BWB projects may include workshops, youth programming, skill-sharing events, civic education, repair skills, emergency preparedness, or hands-on learning.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include schools, libraries, colleges, youth organizations, makerspaces, trade groups, arts organizations, and community centers.

### Sustainability and Circular Systems

BWB's culture often overlaps with reuse, repair, material repurposing, creative fabrication, and practical infrastructure.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include makerspaces, reuse centers, tool libraries, climate groups, environmental nonprofits, neighborhood resilience hubs, repair cafes, and local businesses with relevant equipment or materials.

### Community Convening

Chapters can create welcoming spaces for people to meet, coordinate, prototype ideas, and build local trust.

Strong collaborators for this work usually include neighborhood associations, community centers, libraries, civic groups, arts organizations, and local organizers with existing relationships.

## Collaboration Principles

The most useful collaborations are those that increase local legitimacy, practical capacity, safety, and community ownership without pulling the chapter away from BWB's mission.

### Start With Local Need

Chapters should begin with what residents, community leaders, and existing local organizations already identify as useful. The chapter's role should be to add capacity, creativity, volunteers, logistics, or connective tissue, not to impose a preselected project.

### Support Existing Trust

Chapters should look for organizations and leaders that already have trust in the relevant community. This is especially important when working with people directly affected by disasters, displacement, poverty, exclusion, or public-health risks.

### Avoid Duplication

BWB chapters are usually strongest when they support, amplify, or connect existing work. A chapter should be cautious about creating a parallel service when a local organization is already doing the work well.

### Match Partner Type to Project Need

The best partner is the one that contributes a capability the chapter does not already have. That might be a facility, public authority, technical expertise, community trust, materials, funding, volunteers, safety protocols, or long-term service infrastructure.

### Clarify Roles Early

Even informal collaborations benefit from clear roles. For projects involving money, facilities, recurring commitments, safety risks, participant data, public communications, or shared branding, roles should be written down in an appropriate lightweight agreement.

### Preserve Community Voice

Chapters should not claim to speak for a community unless that community has clearly authorized them to do so. Where possible, affected residents and local leaders should help define the work, not only receive the result.

## Operational Considerations

This document is background context, not legal advice. Still, several operational considerations should guide later implementation work.

- **Legal authority**: Clarify whether the chapter, Burning Man Project, a partner nonprofit, or a fiscal sponsor is the correct entity for agreements, fundraising, insurance, or funds handling.
- **Safety**: Projects involving tools, builds, disaster sites, youth, unhoused neighbors, food, transportation, or overnight activity need explicit safety planning.
- **Volunteer management**: Volunteers need clear roles, supervision, expectations, and escalation paths.
- **Communications**: Public messaging should accurately describe each partner's role and should not imply endorsement beyond the actual project.
- **Data and privacy**: Avoid collecting personal information unless it is necessary, consented to, protected, and assigned to a responsible entity.
- **Mission fit**: Partnerships should expand community benefit without letting sponsors, agencies, or institutions override locally defined needs.
